### Truth 1 — The Old World

**GM:** The Ironlands are not the first home. Somewhere behind your people is a crossing — water, distance, a before. What was it? Why did they leave, and what do they know about where they came from?

**Player:** The crossing was a punishment — our ancestors were exiled. The old world still exists and they remember us.

**GM:** That's specific and it's heavy. Not refugees, not explorers — convicts. The old world didn't collapse, didn't burn. It simply decided your people were no longer welcome in it, and now it continues, across water nobody can cross, carrying its memory of them.

**Locked.** *The crossing was a sentence: the ancestors were exiled, not refugees. The old world endures and still holds their names, and the ocean between them cannot be crossed.*

### Truth 2 — Iron

**GM:** Iron is what oaths are sworn on, what weapons are made from, what gives the Ironlands their name. In your world — where does it come from, and what does it cost?

**Player:** Iron is grown in underground fungal networks. The fungi track debts of a different kind — broken promises of harvest, not oaths.

**GM:** So not a vow sworn before witnesses, not a warrior's oath — the iron remembers agricultural promises. A farmer pledges to tend a field, abandons it, and the iron in their home begins to fail. The metal itself is a material extension of that accounting.

**Locked.** *Iron is grown by underground fungal networks that track broken promises of cultivation; abandon a field you swore to tend and the iron in your home will be eaten from within by pale filaments.*

### Truth 3 — Legacies

**GM:** When your people arrived, was the land empty — or had someone been here before?

**GM:** That's a very different kind of prior settlement. Not ruins from catastrophe — ruins from deliberate, careful removal. The roads are intact. The foundations are level. Whatever happened here was not destruction.

**Locked.** *The Ironlands were inhabited before; roads still run between foundations that hold no walls, and whatever removed the prior settlers left everything standing, as if expecting their return.*

**GM:** That's quietly unsettling. The current settlers think they made choices about where to build, where to connect — but they didn't. They traced a pattern that was already there. Their community network is a copy of the prior one, and they don't know it.

**Locked.** *Settlements occupy the prior foundations and are linked by the prior roads; the community network is a faithful replica of whatever came before, and no one chose it.*

### Truth 6 — Defense

**GM:** How do these communities protect themselves? Who fights when something comes?

**Player:** There are no dedicated warriors. Defense is the obligation of whoever is most indebted to the community that season. Fight the wolves, mend the wall, stand the night watch — that's how you pay what you owe. The most indebted person is usually also the most capable defender.

**GM:** So capability and debt accumulate together. A skilled fighter probably got that way by being deeply indebted for a long time. There's something almost elegant in how the system shapes people.

**Locked.** *Defense is a form of debt repayment; whoever owes the most to the community that season fights its wolves, tends its walls, and stands its watches — which is why the most capable defenders are usually the most indebted.*

**Locked.** *The dead are composted into the soil with ceremony, fed to the fungal network deliberately; grief and cultivation are one practice, and the iron that grows from the network carries something of those who were given to it.*

### Truth 10 — Beasts

**GM:** What creatures threaten the Ironlands?

**Player:** Animals that eat iron-fruiting fungi heavily become extensions of the network. Still themselves, still predators — but navigating by the same debt-logic as the land. A wolf attacking livestock near an abandoned farm is acting on a wound in the network, not hunger. Killing it doesn't fix the wound.

**GM:** That's a beautifully specific problem. The threat is never just the wolf. Behind the wolf is an unhealed debt, an untended field, a broken promise. The settlers who understand this can stop attacks before they happen by keeping cultivation promises. Those who don't keep killing wolves and wondering why more come.

**Locked.** *Animals that feed heavily on iron-fruiting fungi become partial extensions of the network, acting on its debt-logic rather than hunger; they gather at abandoned farms and broken promises, and killing them cannot heal the wound that sent them.*

### Truth 11 — Darkness

**GM:** We know the prior settlers vanished — cleanly, deliberately, leaving their infrastructure intact. We know the network is ancient and purposeful. What is the deepest threat? What shadow lies over the Ironlands?

**Player:** The prior settlers didn't leave because of anything terrible. The network asked them to — and they went. Whatever it asked them to do, they did it. Now the network is asking again, in the same pattern of iron failures and beast movements and debt-tremors. No one has realized this is a summons, not a catastrophe.

**GM:** The horror isn't that something is destroying the world. The horror is that something is *inviting* it, in a language nobody reads, and the last group of people who heard this invitation and understood it simply — went. Where, nobody knows. Whether they came back, nobody knows. Whether coming back was an option, nobody knows.

**Locked.** *The iron failures, the beast movements, and the debt-tremors are not signs of catastrophe — they are the network's summons; the prior settlers heard it too, obeyed it, and vanished, and no one yet understands that what is coming is an invitation.*

## World Summary

**GM:** Here is what is true.

Your ancestors were exiled — sentenced to the Ironlands while the world that cast them out endures across uncrossable water. Iron here is not mined but grown, by fungal networks beneath the soil that track broken promises of cultivation and rot weapons from within when their owners abandon a field they swore to tend. When your people arrived, they found the Ironlands already shaped: roads running between intact foundations, a settlement network already drawn. They built on those foundations and followed those roads, and the current world is a faithful copy of whatever came before.

Authority belongs to whoever fed the most people in the last scarcity. Defense is paid as debt — the most indebted person stands the wall and faces the wolves, which is also why the most capable defenders are usually the most indebted. Those who learn to listen to the mycelium receive the land's memory directly — not visions, but bruises, the felt record of every broken cultivation promise within their range.

The dead are composted into the soil with ceremony. Grief and planting are one practice. The iron that grows from the network carries something of those given to it.

The Firstborn are not creatures that inhabit the Ironlands. They are the land's nervous system — distributed, ancient, incapable of language, in conversation with the settlers through every act of cultivation and every burial. The settlers have never recognized this conversation, but it is ongoing.

Animals that have eaten deeply of iron-fruiting fungi follow the network's debt-logic rather than their own hunger. They are not corrupted so much as conscripted — instruments of the network's accounting, gathering at wounds in the land.

And the prior settlers did not die or flee. The network asked them something, in the pattern of iron failures and beast movements and debt-tremors now appearing again, and they obeyed, and they were never seen again. What is coming for the current settlers is not catastrophe. It is a summons. No one has understood this yet.
