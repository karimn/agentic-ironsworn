## Opening

**GM:** The Ironlands are waiting. Cold, wide, and older than anyone alive. Let's find out what kind of world yours is — one truth at a time. I'll offer you three ways the world might be. Pick the one that feels right, add anything you like, and we'll lock it in. Eleven truths. We'll build as we go.

**Player:** Ready.

## Truth 1 — The Old World

**GM:** Before the Ironlands, there was somewhere else. Your people crossed the ocean — or something like it — and never went back. What do they know about what they left?

- **A** — The crossing is myth. No one remembers why they came or what they left behind. The ocean is uncrossable now; the past is simply gone.
- **B** — They fled something. War, plague, a darkness that consumed their homeland. The details have blurred but the fear hasn't.
- **C** — They were driven out. Another people — or something worse — took what was theirs. The old world still exists; they just can't return to it.

Or something else entirely?

**Player:** B. They fled something. I want the fear to be real but nameless — old people flinch when ships are sighted on the horizon, but nobody can say why anymore.

**GM:** The horizon is where the old dread lives. Children ask what's out there; their grandparents change the subject.

**Locked.** They fled something that consumed the old world — plague, darkness, or worse — and though the details have eroded to flinches and silences, the fear crossed the ocean with them and never left.

type: truth

The crossing happened generations ago and the details have dissolved into flinches and silences. Old folk look away when ships appear on the horizon. Children who ask what lies beyond the ocean are not answered."
aliases: ["The Old World", "Truth: The Old World", "First Truth", "the crossing", "why they came"]

## Truth 2 — Iron

**GM:** Iron is the currency of oaths here — you swear on it, you carry it, you're buried with it. But where does it come from, and what weight does it carry beyond the physical?

- **A** — Mined from the earth at great cost. The veins run deep and the work kills people, but there's no curse in it — just labor and loss.
- **B** — Excavated from ruins left by whoever came before. The metal itself is strange; oaths sworn on it are tracked by the elves who built those ruins. Break one and see what comes.
- **C** — Rare, fought over, hoarded by the powerful. No mystical quality — just scarcity making it worth dying for.

**Player:** B. The ruin-iron. I love that there are elves watching. Makes every oath feel like it has a witness you didn't invite.

**GM:** Every piece of iron you carry was pulled from a place that predates your people's arrival. And somewhere, something has been keeping score since before your grandmother's grandmother was born.

**Locked.** Iron is excavated from ancient ruins of elven make, and oaths sworn on it are witnessed by the elves who built those ruins — break one, and the debt is not merely social.

```upsert_lore
canonical: "Truth 2 — Iron"
type: truth
summary: "Iron comes from ruins that predate the Ironlanders' arrival — places of elven construction, partially buried, partially forgotten. The metal carries something of its origin: oaths sworn on ruin-iron are tracked by the elves who built those places. Breaking such an oath does not merely cost reputation. What it does cost is not widely understood, which may be the point."

## Truth 3 — Legacies

**GM:** We already know humans weren't alone here before your people arrived — the ruins are everywhere, and the elves built them. But were your people the *first humans* in the Ironlands? Or did others come before, and what happened to them?

*(Adaptive note: since Iron established elven ruins as the source of iron, the existence of a pre-human presence is already locked. The real question here is whether earlier humans also came — and whether any trace remains.)*

- **A** — Your people were the first humans. The land was wilderness before them; the ruins are entirely elven, no human hand in them.
- **B** — Others came before. Human foundations, human tunnels, stretches of human road that go nowhere. They built something. Then they vanished. No one knows why.
- **C** — The old settlers never fully vanished. In the deep wilds, communities descended from whoever came before still survive — changed, distant, and unwelcoming to newcomers.

**Player:** B. Abandoned roads and half-collapsed foundations everywhere. Humans built here once and then just... stopped. I want that mystery to hang over everything.

**GM:** You walk a road your people didn't build, toward a settlement your people didn't found, over the bones of a civilization that nobody remembers erasing.

**Locked.** Humans came before — their roads, foundations, and tunnels lace the Ironlands — but whatever they built, they stopped building it, and no one alive knows why.

```upsert_lore
canonical: "Truth 3 — Legacies"
type: truth
summary: "The Ironlands bear the marks of earlier human habitation: roads that lead nowhere, half-buried foundations, tunnels dug by hands that weren't elven. These First People built something in the Ironlands and then ceased entirely. No descendant community survives — or none has been found. The reason for their disappearance is unknown, and that unknowing is itself a source of dread."
aliases: ["Legacies", "Truth: Legacies", "Third Truth", "the First People", "old roads", "prior settlers"]

## Truth 4 — Communities

**GM:** The Ironlands are not empty. People live here — your people, scratching something like civilization out of hard ground. How do they organize themselves? How do they survive?

- **A** — In scarcity. Small, isolated, fiercely self-reliant. A hundred souls is a large settlement. Everyone knows everyone, and that's both comfort and trap.
- **B** — In tension. Real towns with markets and roads between them — dangerous distances, fragile bonds. Community means the people inside your walls; everyone else is a gamble.
- **C** — In networks. Interdependent settlements, each specializing. When a node goes dark, everyone feels it.

**Player:** A. Small and isolated. I want settlements that feel like they're holding on rather than thriving. And I want that to matter — the stakes are personal because everyone around you is someone you know.

**GM:** Fifty faces around a fire. Every one of them matters because you can't afford to lose any of them.

**Locked.** Settlements are small, isolated, and fiercely self-reliant — a hundred souls counts as a large community — and everyone knows everyone, which is at once the deepest comfort and the sharpest trap.

```upsert_lore
canonical: "Truth 4 — Communities"

Each community survives through fierce self-reliance, with little trade and dangerous distances between neighbors. Everyone knows everyone. That intimacy is a comfort and a constraint: every loss is personal, every betrayal is catastrophic, and every stranger is an event."

## Truth 5 — Leaders

**GM:** In a place this isolated, authority is intimate. Who leads, and how? What grants one person the right to tell others what to do?

- **A** — By eldership and consensus. No formal titles. Authority is earned slowly and lost quickly. Everything important is decided together.
- **B** — By iron vow. Leaders are those who have sworn the most and delivered on it. A chieftain who breaks a vow doesn't just lose respect — they lose everything.
- **C** — By strength. Warlords and their sworn warriors hold most settlements by force or the credible threat of it.

**Player:** B, but with a detail — given that the iron itself is elven and watched, I want a leader's vow-breaking to carry a supernatural consequence, not just a social one. The community fears it as much as they fear losing their chieftain.

**GM:** You've made something sharp here. The leader's authority and the iron's witness are the same institution. When a chieftain breaks a vow, the community doesn't just lose its head — it may have announced itself to whatever the elves send.

**Locked.** Authority belongs to those who have sworn the most and kept every oath — a chieftain who breaks a vow loses not only standing but invites the elves' reckoning, and the community shares in that terror.

```upsert_lore
canonical: "Truth 5 — Leaders"
type: truth
summary: "Leadership in the Ironlands flows from the accumulation and keeping of iron vows. Those who swear most and deliver on every oath earn the trust of their community. A chieftain who breaks an oath does not merely lose standing — because iron is elven-made and elven-watched, the breach may draw a reckoning that falls on the whole settlement. This makes the act of leadership inseparable from the act of oath-taking, and makes every community quietly complicit in monitoring their leader's integrity."

## Truth 6 — Defense

**GM:** The Ironlands are dangerous. The wilds push back. Other people sometimes do too. How do these small, isolated settlements protect themselves?

- **A** — Militia and walls. Every able body trains. Settlements are fortified.
- **B** — Cunning and concealment. Walls invite attack — better not to be found. Defense means early warning, knowing the land, and fast retreat.
- **C** — Bonded warriors. Small groups of sworn fighters who have vowed to protect their community above all else. When they fall, the settlement usually falls with them.

**Player:** C. I want the bonded warriors. Given the oath-weight we've established, a warrior who swears to protect a community is making a particularly serious commitment. And it means their death is more than tactical — it's a crack in the world.

**GM:** A warrior's death-vow here is not metaphor. It's a contract with the iron. Every settlement with bonded protectors knows what it costs to call on them.

**Locked.** Small groups of sworn warriors, bound by iron vow to their community's survival, stand as its only true defense — and when they fall, the settlement loses not just its shield but the weight of the oaths that held it together.

```upsert_lore
canonical: "Truth 6 — Defense"
type: truth
summary: "Settlements do not maintain general militias. Defense rests on small groups of bonded warriors who have sworn, on ruin-iron, to protect their community above all else. These vows are witnessed by the elves as surely as any other oath on that metal. When a bonded warrior dies, the community loses its protector and a piece of its elven-witnessed covenant. A settlement whose sworn defenders are gone is often a settlement that doesn't survive the next season."
aliases: ["Defense", "Truth: Defense", "Sixth Truth", "bonded warriors", "sworn defenders", "protectors"]

## Truth 7 — Mysticism

**GM:** The Ironlands are old and strange. Are there people who can touch something beyond the physical — and if so, how does the world understand them?

- **A** — No. The world is hard and physical. Those who claim otherwise are selling something.
- **B** — Yes, but rare, dangerous, and poorly understood. A few people seem to touch something beyond the physical. Communities regard them with uneasy gratitude.
- **C** — Magic is woven into the land. Those who learn to listen — really listen — can draw on it. It's subtle, slow, and costly.

*(Adaptive note: Iron established that elves track oaths on ruin-metal. That's already a form of the uncanny operating in this world. Mysticism here might be: do humans have access to anything like it? Is magic the province of elves alone, or can it be learned/inherited?)*

**Player:** C, but I want to tie it to the land itself — and specifically to the old ruins. People who spend time near the ruins sometimes develop a sensitivity. It's not taught, it's accreted. And it makes communities deeply uneasy because those people start to feel more like whatever came before than like their neighbors.

**GM:** The ruins give as well as take. Those who linger too long near elven stonework come back changed in ways that don't have clean names. They're useful. They're strange. They're not quite gone from their community, but they're no longer entirely *of* it.

**Locked.** Magic is real and woven into the land, strongest near the old ruins — those who spend enough time there sometimes accrete a sensitivity to it, becoming subtly altered, deeply unsettling to their community, and unreachably useful.

```upsert_lore
canonical: "Truth 7 — Mysticism"
type: truth
summary: "Magic exists in the Ironlands, rooted in the land itself and concentrated near elven ruins. It cannot be taught in the conventional sense — it accretes. People who spend significant time in or near ruin-sites sometimes return changed: sensitive to things others cannot perceive, capable of acts that escape physical explanation. Communities regard them with uneasy gratitude. These Ruin-Touched are useful enough to keep close and strange enough to never fully trust. Over time, they feel less like neighbors and more like something the ruins left behind."

## Truth 8 — Religion

**GM:** People in the Ironlands need something to believe in — or maybe belief is just what happens when you live close to death and don't have good answers. What shapes their spiritual life?

- **A** — The Old Gods. Ancient, nameless forces tied to the land — storms, harvests, death. Offerings at old stones, hope that something is listening.
- **B** — One God, distant and silent. Faith requires no evidence and demands no proof.
- **C** — The Dead. Ancestor veneration shapes everything — who you owe, what you're permitted to do. The ancestors are not quiet.

*(Adaptive note: With communities so small and every loss so personal, ancestor veneration has particular weight. And if ancestors are "not quiet," that intersects interestingly with the ruin-touched — are mystics the ones who can actually hear the dead?)*

**Player:** C. The dead. And yes — I want mystics to be the ones who can hear the ancestors most clearly. That's part of why communities keep them close despite the unease. They're the only ones who can relay what the dead want.

**GM:** The dead never fully leave in the Ironlands — and the ruin-touched are the thin place where the living and the dead can still, imperfectly, speak. That makes mystics not just unsettling but necessary in a way that can't be argued away.

**Locked.** The dead are the center of spiritual life — ancestors make demands, issue warnings, and do not rest quietly — and the ruin-touched are often the only ones who can hear them clearly, which is why they are feared and kept.

```upsert_lore
canonical: "Truth 8 — Religion"

The dead make demands, issue warnings, and punish those who break faith with them. This is not metaphor — ancestors are understood to be present, if inaccessible to most. The ruin-touched, with their accreted sensitivity, can often hear the dead more clearly than anyone else. This is a principal reason communities tolerate their strangeness: mystics serve as translators between the living and the dead who refuse to be silent."

## Truth 9 — The Firstborn

**GM:** We already know the elves exist — they built the ruins, and they watch the oaths sworn on iron pulled from those ruins. So the question isn't whether they're real. It's: what are they *like*? How do they actually behave toward humans? Is contact possible? Has it ever happened deliberately, on their side?

*(Adaptive note applied: this question is reframed from "do elder races exist" to "how alien and communicative are the elves.")*

- **A** — Completely opaque. They appear when something breaks — an oath, a ruin disturbed — and then are gone. No human has ever had a real exchange with one.
- **B** — Rare deliberate contact, almost always one-sided. Once or twice in living memory, an elf chose to speak. Never to comfort. Always to warn or demand.
- **C** — In certain deep places, there are communities where humans and elves share space. Not warmly. But something has been exchanged.

**Player:** B. Once or twice in living memory, an elf chose to speak. I want those encounters to be infamous — everyone knows the stories, nobody is sure what to make of them. And the elf always wanted something, or was telling you something you didn't want to hear.

**GM:** There are maybe three such stories anyone can name, and they all have the same shape: stillness, a voice from somewhere you weren't looking, and afterwards the feeling that you'd been assessed and found barely sufficient.

**Locked.** The elves do not seek contact, but once or twice in living memory one has chosen to speak to a human — always to warn, demand, or assess, never to comfort — and these encounters have the character of legend: specific, terrifying, and wholly one-sided.

```upsert_lore
canonical: "Truth 9 — The Firstborn"
type: truth
summary: "The elves — who built the ruins and watch the oaths — do not seek contact with humans. They appear at ruptures: broken oaths, disturbed ruins, moments of threshold significance. On two or three occasions within living memory, an elf has deliberately spoken to a human. These encounters are remembered as legends: the elf appeared without warning, said something that could not be mistaken for kindness, and left. No human has ever successfully initiated contact. No exchange has ever felt mutual."

## Truth 10 — Beasts

**GM:** The wilds are not safe. Something out there hunts — or worse, doesn't hunt and just *is*. What threatens the Ironlands from the outside?

- **A** — Natural predators. Wolves, bears, things that haven't learned to fear people. Dangerous but comprehensible.
- **B** — Corrupted creatures. Something has touched the wildlife near ruins and old dig sites — what emerges is wrong. Wrong-shaped, wrong-tempered, wrong in ways that escape description.
- **C** — Ancient things. Creatures that predate humanity, vast and indifferent. They don't hunt. They simply exist, and their existence is enough.

*(Adaptive note: We established that humans came before and vanished without explanation. And that magic concentrates near ruins. Corrupted beasts gathering near ruins connect those two threads — the beasts may be a clue to what erased the First People.)*

**Player:** B. The corrupted creatures near ruins. And yes — I want it to be a live question whether the corruption is connected to why the First People disappeared. The beasts near ruins aren't just dangerous; they're evidence of something.

**GM:** A creature that has spent too long near ruin-stone isn't just twisted — it's a data point. The same thing that gives mystics their sensitivity may be what unmakes the animals entirely. Something in those ruins doesn't differentiate between learning and consuming.

**Locked.** Creatures that spend too long near ruins become corrupted — wrong in shape and behavior, driven by something beyond hunger — and the community suspicion that this same force unmade the First People is not easily dismissed.

## Truth 11 — Darkness

**GM:** We've built a world with a mystery at its heart: elven ruins that change everything they touch, a people who fled something and don't know what, humans who came before and vanished. Now name the shadow. What is the deepest threat — the thing that, if no one does anything, will consume the Ironlands?

- **A** — A conquering force. An external enemy is expanding. Settlements fall; the Ironlands are contracting.
- **B** — Rot from within. No external enemy. Communities turn on each other — resources thin, trust collapses, old wounds reopen.
- **C** — Something waking. Deep in the oldest ruins, something dormant is becoming less so. The corrupted beasts, the strange mystics, the elves' behavior — all connected. Something is coming and nobody has words for it yet.

*(Adaptive note: Given what we've built — elves watching oaths, ruins that corrupt and change, the mystery of the First People — option C is the thematic culmination. But the player may surprise us.)*

**Player:** C. Something waking. And I want the thread to be explicit: the reason the First People disappeared is the same reason the beasts are changing, the same reason the elves are watching so carefully, and the same reason the ancestors won't be quiet. Something under the oldest ruins was dormant when humans first arrived, and it isn't anymore.

**GM:** The elves didn't build the ruins to store iron. They built them to contain something. Your people have been mining the containment walls.

**Locked.** Something ancient and vast stirs beneath the oldest ruins — the corrupted beasts, the ruin-touched mystics, the ancestors' warnings, the elves' watchfulness all point toward the same source — and the First People likely vanished when this thing last woke.

```upsert_lore

The corrupted beasts, the accreted sensitivity of the ruin-touched, the unquiet ancestors, and the elves' rare and terrifying interventions are all symptoms of the same stirring. The First People who vanished from the Ironlands almost certainly encountered this force when it last woke — and were erased by it. The elven ruins were not merely habitations; they were, in some sense, containment. The Ironlanders have been stripping them for iron without knowing what they were unmating."

## World Summary

**GM:** Here is the world we've built.

---

The Ironlanders fled something that consumed their homeland — plague, darkness, or a force without a name — and though the memory has eroded to flinches and silences, the fear crossed the ocean with them and never left. Iron comes from ruins of elven make, and every oath sworn on it is watched by the elves who built those places; break one, and the debt is not merely social. Earlier humans also lived here — their roads and foundations lace the land — but they stopped entirely, and no one alive knows why. Settlements are small, isolated, and fiercely self-reliant, a hundred souls at most, and everyone knows everyone, which is at once the deepest comfort and the sharpest trap. Authority belongs to those who have sworn the most and kept every oath, and a chieftain who breaks a vow may invite the elves' reckoning on the entire community. Defense rests on small groups of sworn warriors bound by iron vow to their settlement's survival, and when they fall, the settlement usually falls with them. Magic is woven into the land and concentrates near ruins; those who spend too long there accrete a sensitivity that marks them as changed, deeply unsettling, and unreachably useful. The dead are the center of spiritual life — ancestors make demands and do not rest quietly — and the ruin-touched are often the only ones who can hear them clearly, which is why they are feared and kept. The elves do not seek contact, but once or twice in living memory one has spoken to a human, always to warn or demand, never to comfort, and these encounters have the character of legend. Creatures that spend too long near ruins become corrupted — wrong in shape and temper — and the suspicion that this same force erased the First People is not easily dismissed. Beneath the oldest ruins, something ancient stirs: the corrupted beasts, the ruin-touched mystics, the unquiet dead, and the elves' careful watchfulness all point toward the same source — and the First People likely vanished when this thing last woke.

---

**GM:** Does anything feel wrong or missing?

**Player:** No. That's the world. Let's play.
